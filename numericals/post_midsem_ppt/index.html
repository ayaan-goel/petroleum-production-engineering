Problem 01

Finding Flowing Bottomhole Pressure

Problem Statement

Graphical Solution

Analysis & Methodology

Objective: Find the flowing pressure at the foot (\( P_{wf} \)) of \( 13,000 \text{ ft} \) of \( 2 \frac{3}{8} \text{ in.} \) tubing. The well is flowing \( 100 \text{ bbl/day} \) at a Gas-Liquid Ratio (\( GLR \)) of \( 1 \text{ mcf/bbl} \), with a given Tubing Head Pressure (\( P_{wh} \)) of \( 200 \text{ psi} \).

Given:

  • \( L = 13,000 \text{ ft} \)
  • \( q = 100 \text{ bbl/day} \)
  • \( GLR = 1.0 \text{ mcf/bbl} \)
  • \( P_{wh} = 200 \text{ psi} \)

Step-by-Step Procedure:

  1. Locate THP: Start at the top pressure axis and locate the Tubing Head Pressure of \( 200 \text{ psi} \) (marked as ①).
  2. Find Equivalent Depth: Drop vertically down from \( 200 \text{ psi} \) to intersect the gradient curve corresponding to \( GLR = 1.0 \text{ mcf/bbl} \) (marked as ②).
  3. Read the equivalent depth horizontally on the left axis. The equivalent depth for the wellhead is approximately \( 3,000 \text{ ft} \) (marked as ③).
  4. Add Tubing Length: Add the actual length of the tubing to this equivalent depth to find the equivalent depth of the bottom of the well.
    \( L_{eq, bottom} = 3,000 \text{ ft} + 13,000 \text{ ft} = 16,000 \text{ ft} \)
    (marked as ④).
  5. Locate New Point: Move horizontally to the right from the new depth of \( 16,000 \text{ ft} \) to intersect the same GLR curve (\( 1.0 \text{ mcf/bbl} \)) (marked as ⑤).
  6. Read BHP: Move vertically upward from this intersection point to read the corresponding pressure on the top axis.
Final Result: The flowing bottomhole pressure (\( P_{wf} \)) is approximately \( 1850 \text{ psi} \) (marked as ⑥).
Problem 02

Finding Tubing Head Pressure (THP)

Problem Statement

Graphical Solution

Analysis & Methodology

Objective: Determine the Tubing Head Pressure (\( P_{wh} \)) for a well completed with \( 8,000 \text{ ft} \) of \( 2 \frac{3}{8} \text{ in.} \) tubing. The well is flowing at \( 600 \text{ bbl/day} \), with a GLR of \( 0.4 \text{ mcf/bbl} \), and the bottomhole flowing pressure is \( 2200 \text{ psi} \).

Given:

  • \( L = 8,000 \text{ ft} \)
  • \( q = 600 \text{ bbl/day} \)
  • \( GLR = 0.4 \text{ mcf/bbl} \)
  • \( P_{wf} = 2200 \text{ psi} \)

Step-by-Step Procedure:

  1. Locate BHP: Start at the top pressure axis and locate the given Bottomhole Pressure of \( 2200 \text{ psi} \) (marked as ①).
  2. Find Equivalent Depth: Drop vertically down to intersect the gradient curve corresponding to \( GLR = 0.4 \text{ mcf/bbl} \) (marked as ②).
  3. Move horizontally to the left axis to read the equivalent depth. The equivalent depth at the bottom of the well is approximately \( 12,000 \text{ ft} \) (marked as ③).
  4. Subtract Tubing Length: Subtract the actual tubing length from the bottom equivalent depth to find the equivalent depth of the wellhead.
    \( L_{eq, wellhead} = 12,000 \text{ ft} - 8,000 \text{ ft} = 4,000 \text{ ft} \)
    (marked as ④).
  5. Locate Wellhead Point: Move horizontally to the right from the new depth of \( 4,000 \text{ ft} \) to intersect the same GLR curve (\( 0.4 \text{ mcf/bbl} \)) (marked as ⑤).
  6. Read THP: Move vertically upward from this intersection point to read the pressure on the top axis.
Final Result: The Tubing Head Pressure (\( P_{wh} \)) is approximately \( 530 \text{ psi} \).
Problem 03

Will the Well Flow?

Problem Statement

Graphical Solution

Analysis & Methodology

Objective: Determine if a well will flow at a desired rate given reservoir and tubing constraints.

Given:

  • \( J \,(\text{Productivity Index}) = 0.4 \text{ bbl/day/psi} \)
  • \( \bar{P}_R \,(\text{Static Pressure}) = 1500 \text{ psi} \)
  • \( q = 400 \text{ bbl/day} \)
  • \( L = 4000 \text{ ft} \)
  • \( GLR = 0.2 \text{ mcf/bbl} \)

Step 1: Calculate the Available Bottomhole Pressure (Inflow)

Using the Productivity Index equation:

\( q = J \times (\bar{P}_R - P_{wf}) \implies P_{wf, available} = \bar{P}_R - \frac{q}{J} \)

\( P_{wf, available} = 1500 - \frac{400}{0.4} = 1500 - 1000 = \mathbf{500 \text{ psi}} \)


Step 2: Determine the Required Bottomhole Pressure (Outflow)

Assuming a standard Tubing Head Pressure (\( P_{wh} \)) of \( 200 \text{ psi} \) (as indicated by the solution markings):

  1. Start at \( P_{wh} = 200 \text{ psi} \) on the top axis (marked as ①).
  2. Drop vertically to the \( GLR = 0.2 \text{ mcf/bbl} \) curve (marked as ②).
  3. Read the equivalent wellhead depth on the left axis: \( 3,500 \text{ ft} \) (marked as ③).
  4. Add the tubing length (\( 4,000 \text{ ft} \)) to find the bottom equivalent depth:
    \( L_{eq, bottom} = 3,500 \text{ ft} + 4,000 \text{ ft} = \mathbf{7,500 \text{ ft}} \)
    (marked as ④).
  5. Move right from \( 7,500 \text{ ft} \) to intersect the \( 0.2 \) GLR curve (marked as ⑤) and read the pressure vertically on the top axis.
  6. The Required \( P_{wf} \) to lift the fluid to surface against a \( 200 \text{ psi} \) THP is approximately \( 500 \text{ psi} \).

Step 3: Compare Available vs. Required Pressure

The reservoir can supply \( 500 \text{ psi} \) at the wellbore, and the tubing requires approximately \( 500 \text{ psi} \) to lift that fluid to the surface.

Final Result: Since \( P_{wf, available} \, (500 \text{ psi}) \approx P_{wf, required} \, (500 \text{ psi}) \), the well is at a borderline/equilibrium condition and CAN flow at the desired rate of \( 400 \text{ bbl/day} \).
