💡 Why Artificial Lift? When reservoir energy is insufficient to lift fluids to surface naturally, artificial lift adds external energy via subsurface pumps, gas injection, or surface compression.
Q1 Sucker Rod Pump – Surface Unit Components 10 Marks
"Illustrate any five (5) components of the surface unit of a Sucker Rod Pump used in the oil and gas industry."

🔑 Overview

SRP (also called Beam Pumping Unit or Nodding Donkey) is the oldest and most widely used artificial lift. It converts rotary motion → reciprocating motion to lift oil.

Two types of surface units: Conventional (walking beam = Class-I lever) and Non-conventional (walking beam = Class-III lever).

Fig 1: SRP Surface Unit Components

⚙️ 10 Key Components (Pick any 5 + describe)

  1. Prime Mover – Electric motor or IC engine; provides the driving energy
  2. V-belt Drive – Transmits rotary motion from prime mover to gear reducer
  3. Double Reduction Gearbox – Reduces high-speed low-torque to low-speed high-torque output
  4. Crank Arm – Connected to crankshaft; has holes for pitman arm attachment to vary stroke length
  5. Pitman Arm – Connecting rod that converts crank's rotary motion → reciprocating up/down motion
  6. Counterweight – Attached to crank arms; reduces energy needed during upstroke
  7. Walking Beam – Central lever that transmits energy from prime mover to sucker rod string
  8. Sampson Post – Steel support legs that hold the walking beam, horsehead, and associated loads
  9. Horse Head (Mulehead) – Attached to walking beam end; keeps polished rod directly over wellhead
  10. Polished Rod – Topmost rod; passes through stuffing box and connects to sucker rod string
  11. Stuffing Box – Replaces crown valve in SRP wells; rubber packing prevents fluid leakage
  12. Equalizer – Connects pitman arm to walking beam via equalizer bearing
✨ Bluff Tip

Mention: "The counterweight is critical for energy balance — during upstroke it rotates downward reducing motor load, and stores energy during downstroke." Then draw a neat labelled diagram.

Q2 SRP – Pumping Cycle (Subsurface Pump) 10 Marks
"Appraise on the pumping cycle of a sub-surface pump of a sucker rod pump used in the oil and gas industry."

🔩 Subsurface Pump Components

  • Barrel (Working Barrel/Liner) – cylindrical housing suspended on tubing
  • Plunger – moves up and down inside barrel; driven by sucker rod
  • Standing Valve – stationary ball-and-seat valve at bottom of barrel; allows upward flow only
  • Travelling Valve – in the plunger; allows upward flow only
  • Pump Seat – holds the standing valve in position
Fig 2: SRP Subsurface Pump & Pumping Cycle

🔄 Downstroke

  • Plunger moves downward
  • Travelling valve → OPENS (fluid enters plunger from below)
  • Standing valve → CLOSES (weight of fluid column overcomes BHP)
  • Fluid passes through travelling valve into the space above plunger

🔼 Upstroke

  • Plunger moves upward
  • Travelling valve → CLOSES (fluid weight above prevents backflow)
  • Standing valve → OPENS (suction pressure < BHP, so well fluid enters barrel)
  • Fluid above plunger is lifted toward surface; new fluid enters barrel from formation
🔑 Both valves are unidirectional — they only allow upward flow. This is the key principle of the pumping cycle.
✨ Bluff Tip

Draw a 2-panel diagram: left panel = downstroke (TV open, SV closed), right panel = upstroke (TV closed, SV open). Label fluid direction arrows. This alone can get you 7/10.

Q3 Gas Lift System & Types 15 Marks
"Illustrate on the Gas Lift System and the types of Gas Lift used in the oil and gas industry with a neat diagram."

🔑 Principle

High-pressure gas is injected into the fluid column in the tubing to reduce the density of the fluid from the point of injection to the surface. This allows a lower reservoir pressure to lift fluids to surface.

Fig 3: Gas Lift System

Type 1: Continuous Gas Lift

  • Gas is injected continuously at a controlled rate to aerate the liquid column
  • Reservoir must have good BHP and Productivity Index
  • Only one operating valve (as deep as possible) does the injection; upper valves are unloading valves (closed once injection begins)
  • Gas injection through GLV port sizes: 3/16", 1/4", 5/16"
  • Best for: High volume, continuous production

Type 2: Intermittent Gas Lift

  • Large volume of gas injected quickly under a liquid slug, then injection stops
  • Gas expands and displaces oil slug to surface; idle period allows liquid build-up
  • Applicable for wells with: High PI, low BHP Low PI, low BHP Low PI, high BHP
✨ Bluff Tip

Mention: "The premature application of gas lift used the U-tube principle for shallow wells. With advent of Gas Lift Valves (GLVs), application extended to deeper wells." This shows depth of knowledge.

Q4 Gas Lift Valve – Injection Pressure Derivation 15 Marks
"Derive the equation for Gas Lift injection pressure with a neat diagram labelling the components."

🔩 GLV Components

A GLV has 5 basic components: Body, Loading Element, Responsive Element, Transmission Element, Metering Element. The most common is the bellow-operated nitrogen-pressure loaded GLV.

Fig 4: GLV Force Balance Diagram

📐 Derivation

Let: Pinj = injection pressure, Pdome = dome pressure, Pprod = production pressure, Ab = bellows area, AP = port area

Force Balance for valve to open:

Pinj×(Ab−AP) + Pprod×AP ≥ Ab×Pdome

Rearranging:

Pinj ≥ (Pdome − Pprod×M) / (1 − M)

Where M = AP/Ab (port-to-bellows area ratio)

🔑 The valve opens when injection pressure + production pressure ≥ dome pressure. The dome is pre-charged with nitrogen at a set pressure.
✨ Bluff Tip

State: "In practice, M is small (AP ≪ Ab), so the valve is primarily controlled by injection pressure. The dome pressure acts as the closing force via the bellows."

Q5 & Q6 Progressive Cavity Pump (PCP) 15 Marks each
"Discuss the operating principles / Illustrate the PCP with a diagram labelling its parts and discuss advantages and limitations."

🔑 Introduction

PCP is the youngest artificial lift method (used since early 1980s). Used mainly for: Heavy oil CBM dewatering Sand cuts up to 85% Mature water floods

Fig 5: PCP Assembly
Fig 6: PCP Operating Principle – Rotor & Stator

⚙️ Operating Principle

  • Consists of two helical shafts: metal Rotor (inner) inside rubber Stator (outer)
  • Rotor has one less lobe than the stator; every tooth of rotor is always in contact with stator inner surface
  • As rotor rotates inside stator, sealed cavities form and move axially from suction to discharge → creates pumping action
  • Two conditions for closed cavities: (1) rotor has one fewer tooth than stator, (2) they constitute longitudinally two helical gears
  • Result: uniform, non-pulsating flow; reversible and self-priming

✅ Advantages

  • Low capital and operating costs
  • Handles high viscous fluids, large solid concentrations
  • No valves to clog or gas lock
  • High tolerance for free gas (no pulsation)
  • Low wellhead profile

❌ Limitations

  • Max depth ~8,000 ft (2000 m)
  • Max temperature ~150°C (elastomer degrades beyond this)
  • Max rate ~250 m³/day
  • Low volumetric efficiency with large gas volumes
  • Requires constant fluid level above pump (no dry run)
  • Rod/coupling wear in deviated wells
✨ Bluff Tip

Mention: "PCP is extensively used in Canada, China, and Venezuela for heavy oil, and in ~1000 CBM wells in USA for dewatering. It is particularly popular in environmentally sensitive areas due to its low wellhead profile."

Q7 & Q8 Electric Submersible Pump (ESP) 15 + 10 Marks
"Appraise on the ESP with a labelled diagram / Discuss advantages and disadvantages of ESP."

🔑 Introduction

ESP is a high-volume artificial lift for lifting large volumes of fluid from great depths. It is economical and effective under a variety of well conditions.

Fig 7: ESP System Components

⚙️ 5 Distinct Components

  1. Pump Section – Multi-stage centrifugal pump; OD and impeller design determines flow rate; number of stages determines head. BHP = (Q × Head × SG) / Pump Efficiency. Motor type: two-pole, three-phase squirrel cage induction (3500 rpm @ 60Hz)
  2. Separator Section (Intake) – Path for fluid to enter pump; often a gas separator (poor-boy type or rotary type centrifuge) to remove free gas before it enters pump
  3. Seal Section (Protector) – Protects motor by: (a) allowing expansion/contraction of dielectric oil, (b) equalizing annulus pressure with motor fluid, (c) isolating well fluid from motor oil, (d) absorbing pump thrust via sliding bearing
  4. Subsurface Motor – Two-pole, three-phase squirrel cage induction motor; filled with refined mineral oil for dielectric strength and thermal conductivity; attached at bottom of assembly
  5. Subsurface Cable – Three-phase power cable banded to production tubing; must withstand wellbore T, P, and resist well fluid impregnation; high voltage = low current = small conductor diameter (<0.25 in)

Motor Shroud – used when fluid flow past motor is insufficient for cooling; diverts all fluid past motor.

✅ Advantages

  • High volume and depth capability
  • Highly efficient
  • Low maintenance
  • Minor surface equipment needed
  • Good in deviated wells
  • Adaptable to automation

❌ Disadvantages

  • Limited adaptability to major reservoir changes
  • Difficult to repair in field (entire string must be pulled)
  • Scale deposits interfere with pump operation
  • High electricity cost in remote areas
  • Limited flexibility under some producing conditions
✨ Bluff Tip

Mention the BHP formula: BHP = (Q × Head × SG) / Pump Efficiency. This shows you know the design considerations. Also note: "ESP is preferred for offshore wells and high water-cut wells where large volumes must be handled efficiently."

Q9 Hydraulic Lift – Jet Pump 15 Marks
"Illustrate on the working of Jet Pump with a diagram labelling its parts and discuss the advantages and limitations."

🔑 Principle

Jet pumps transfer energy from a high-pressure (HP) power fluid to boost pressure of low-pressure (LP) well fluid via momentum transfer through an ejector nozzle, throat, and diffuser. No moving parts downhole.

Fig 8: Jet Pump – Nozzle, Throat, Diffuser

⚙️ Components & Working

  1. Nozzle – HP power fluid passes through; potential energy converts to kinetic energy (velocity ↑, pressure ↓)
  2. Suction Chamber (Production Inlet) – Low pressure at nozzle exit sucks in well fluid from annulus
  3. Mixing Tube (Throat) – Power fluid and well fluid mix; momentum transfer takes place
  4. Diffuser – Mixture decelerates (velocity ↓, pressure ↑); acquires discharge pressure sufficient to lift fluid to surface
🔑 Venturi Effect: When fluid passes through the constricted throat, velocity increases → pressure drops → suction created to draw in well fluid.

✅ Advantages

  • Positive displacement – strong drawdown
  • Double-acting, high volumetric efficiency
  • Good depth/volume capability (>15,000 ft)
  • Operates in deviated wells
  • Multi-well production from single surface package
  • No downhole moving parts

❌ Limitations

  • Solids handling issues
  • Requires specific BHA
  • Medium volume potential only (50–1000 BPD)
  • Requires service facilities
  • Gas handling limitations
  • Requires high-pressure surface line
✨ Bluff Tip

Key differentiator: "Unlike all other artificial lift methods, the jet pump has NO moving parts downhole — the energy transfer is entirely through fluid momentum. This makes it extremely reliable and maintenance-free at depth."
